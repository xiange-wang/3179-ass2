Introduction

In today's modern era, energy consumption stands as a pivotal indicator a nation's development and progress . With the global population on the rise and industrial sectors expanding, the demand for energy has witnessed an unprecedented surge . This visualization will analyze in this.

The relationship between GDP and energy consumption is often viewed as symbiotic. As nations develop and industrialize, their energy demands typically increase, driving up GDP. Conversely, a robust GDP can indicate high energy utilization, reflecting economic activities and growth.

Here, this visualization uses a bar chart to rank countries with higher GDPs globally and highlights the top two countries with a distinct color. In conjunction with the first chart, readers can better compare and analyze the situation of high GDP countries in relation to energy consumption

GDP/capita and Energy/capita

How to Interact: Selected different areas in the second picture

Per capita GDP and per capita energy consumption are closely intertwined. As individual income (GDP/capita) increases, the demand for goods, services, and modern amenities rises, leading to greater energy consumption per person. Conversely, efficient energy infrastructure can boost economic productivity and raise GDP/capita.

Because GDP and total energy are affected by the size of the population, GDP per capita and energy consumption per capita are analyzed here. In general when GDP per capita increases, energy consumption per capita also increases. However, some counties like Iceland will have a relatively high per capita energy consumption due to environmental reasons.

Per capita energy consumption measures the average energy use of each indivuidal in a specific region or country. It provides insights into the lifestyle, economic activities, and technological advancement of that area. A higher consumption can indicate a developed infrastructure and advanced technology, while a lower rate may suggest inefficiencies or limited access to modern energy sources.
